Field Story

How KoboToolbox Is Transforming Humanitarian Response Across East Africa

📅 March 10, 2025 · ✍️ Kobo Editorial Team · ⏱ 8 min read

Over the past five years, humanitarian organizations across East Africa have been using KoboToolbox to revolutionize how they assess needs, register beneficiaries, and monitor aid distribution — often in some of the most challenging environments on Earth.

From South Sudan and Ethiopia to Kenya and Somalia, field teams are collecting data digitally for the first time, replacing paper-based systems that were slow, error-prone, and difficult to scale. The results have been transformative.

The Challenge: Data in Crisis

When a humanitarian crisis unfolds — whether a conflict, a flood, or a drought — the ability to quickly and accurately assess needs is literally a matter of life and death. Aid organizations need to know: Where are people? What do they need? Who has already received assistance? How are conditions changing?

For decades, this data was collected on paper forms that had to be manually transcribed, cleaned, and analyzed — a process that could take days or weeks. In fast-moving emergencies, that's time aid organizations simply don't have.

"When we could finally get real-time data from the field, everything changed. We could see exactly where needs were highest and respond within hours instead of days."

KoboToolbox addresses this challenge by providing a free, open-source platform that allows organizations to design digital data collection forms, collect data offline on mobile devices, and instantly sync and analyze results when connectivity is available.

UNHCR: Registering Millions of Refugees

The UN Refugee Agency (UNHCR) has been using KoboToolbox across its East Africa operations for several years. In countries like Ethiopia, Kenya, Uganda, and Tanzania — which together host millions of refugees — UNHCR field teams use Kobo to:

  • Register new arrivals at refugee reception centers
  • Conduct household vulnerability assessments
  • Monitor shelter conditions and access to basic services
  • Track cash-based assistance distributions
  • Collect data for population monitoring surveys

The ability to collect data offline is critical. Many refugee settlements are in remote areas with limited or no internet connectivity. Field teams collect data throughout the day, then sync automatically when they return to a location with connectivity.

MSF: Real-Time Disease Surveillance

Médecins Sans Frontières (MSF) has deployed KoboToolbox across multiple East Africa operations to strengthen disease surveillance and outbreak response. When cholera outbreaks occur — a common threat in displacement settings — MSF teams use Kobo to track cases in real time, enabling rapid response to contain spread.

The platform's support for skip logic (where questions are shown or hidden based on previous answers) allows MSF to build complex clinical data collection forms that guide field health workers through the right questions for each patient, reducing errors and improving data quality.

Key Features Driving Adoption

Several features of KoboToolbox have been particularly important for humanitarian organizations in East Africa:

  • Offline data collection: Works without internet connectivity using the KoboCollect Android app
  • GPS tracking: Automatically records the location of each data submission
  • Photo and media attachments: Allows field teams to capture images as part of assessments
  • Multilingual support: Forms can be translated into local languages, improving comprehension
  • Real-time dashboards: Data is immediately visible in dashboards once synced
  • Encryption: Sensitive data can be encrypted for protection during transmission

The Numbers Tell the Story

The scale of data collection happening across East Africa through KoboToolbox is remarkable. Organizations across the region are collectively submitting hundreds of thousands of forms per month — data that drives decisions affecting millions of vulnerable people.

For one regional UNHCR operation, switching from paper to KoboToolbox cut the time from data collection to analysis from two weeks to two days. For organizations making critical decisions about where to position supplies or allocate health workers, that difference can save lives.

Looking Ahead

The adoption of digital data tools like KoboToolbox represents a broader shift in how humanitarian organizations work. As organizations build internal capacity to design, deploy, and analyze data collection systems, they become more efficient, more responsive, and more accountable — not just to donors, but to the communities they serve.

Kobo is committed to continuing to develop tools that meet the real-world needs of humanitarian field teams — and to keeping those tools free and accessible for the nonprofits and NGOs that need them most.
